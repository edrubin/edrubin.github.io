When is seeing believing?

Measurement and mismeasurement

Measurement error is not new to economics (or statistics).1

Yet, remote sensing (RS) adds new elements to consider.

  • most RS data are processed; (raw data present their own challenges);
  • RS products are often predictions from models that combine satellite imagery with other features;
  • training/validation data are typically from selected/endogenous samples;
  • actors may strategically respond to RS data (and its errors).

⇒ concerns for identification, inference, and application.

  1. Early stat. work: Adcock (1877/8); Kummell (1879); Gini (1921);
    Early econ. work: Frisch (1934); Koopmans (1937); Wald (1940); Haavelmo (1944); Reiersøl (1950); Durbin (1954)

Challenge #1: Measurement error

\(\hat{x}\) vs. \(x\)

Remotely sensed variables have error.

Current lit might acknowledge this issue—but rarely addresses it.

  • data products created without causal inference in mind;
  • likely not classical measurement error;
  • ⇒ biased point estimates and/or inference;

Potential solutions:1

  • training/validation should match use case;
  • use ground-truth/validation data to debias causal inference.

  1. Of course, with caveats.

Donaldson and Storeygard (2016)

… it is important to recognize that the types of errors that would seriously jeopardize a typical economic application of remotely sensed data are not always a main concern of the remote sensing community.

Measurement error refresh

Setup

Observed value differs from the true value due to some measurement error \[\textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}} = \textcolor{#ffa50040}{\alpha_0} \textcolor{#0000001A}{+} \textcolor{#ffa50040}{\alpha_1} \textcolor{#e64173}{x_\text{true}} + \textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\]

Papers frequently invoke attenuation bias from classical measurement error

  • \(\textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}}\) is an unbiased1 estimate of \(\textcolor{#e64173}{x_\text{true}}\),
  • \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\) is independent of \(\textcolor{#e64173}{x_\text{true}}\),
  • \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\) is independent of any other variables of interest (outcomes or regressors).

Unfortunately, RS likely generates non-classical measurement error.

  1. \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_0} = 0\); \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_1} = 1\); \(E[\textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}] = 0\); and often \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\) has a constant variance.

Measurement error

In remotely sensed variables

If \(\textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}}\) results from a prediction or calibration algorithm
e.g., pollution, income, temp.

  • we often have mean-reverted predictions,
  • overpredicting low values and underpredicting high values,

\[\textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}} = \textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_0} \textcolor{#000000}{+} \textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_1} \textcolor{#e64173}{x_\text{true}} + \textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\]

where \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_0} > 0\) and \(0 < \textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_1} < 1\). ⇒ No guarantee of attenuation bias.1


Categorical/binned variables face a similar problem: Errors correlate with truth.

Measurement error

In remotely sensed variables

Recent work suggests a more prevalent and pernicious type of measurement error1 common to remotely sensed variables.

Differential measurement error

\(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}} + \textcolor{#7cae96}{u} \quad\quad\quad\) model of interest

\(\textcolor{#6a5acd}{x_\text{obs}} = \textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_0} + \textcolor{#ffa500}{\alpha_1} \textcolor{#e64173}{x_\text{true}} + \textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon} \quad\quad\quad\) measurement error

and \(\textcolor{#ffa500}{\epsilon}\) correlates with \(\textcolor{#7cae96}{u}\).

I.e., error correlates with other factors that correlate with the outcome...
… endogenous measurement error.

  1. Proctor, Carleton, and Sum (2026) call this type of measurement error differential measurement error.

Measurement error

Evidence so far

The problem is not “satellites are noisy.”

The problem is that the noise is structured in economically meaningful ways.

  • Systematic parameter bias: Proctor, Carleton, and Sum show remotely sensed variables often fail to recover the target parameter.
  • Treatment-correlated error: Alix-García and Millimet show forest measures can generate nonclassical bias in PES estimates.
  • Binning/classification error: Carson and Yu; Wardle show discretization and misclassification can distort climate, crop, and land-use estimates.1

  1. Related: New work by Jones et al. on binning in climate-response estimation.

Challenge #2: Validation data

A common false dilemma

RS data are often pitched as an alternative to “traditional” data.

Yet, RS-based causal inference relies on (traditional) ground-truth data.

  • part of training/calibration/prediction;
  • part of RS validation/quality control;
  • central to meas.-error solutions.

Challenge Ground-truth data often rely on endog. monitor infrastructures.

  • selection into validation data ⇒ potential downstream bias; (\(-\) ext. validity)
  • RS training/validation approaches often don’t match econ applications;
  • strategic responses to observation further “complicate things.”

Validation issues

Poor upstream/downstream match

Related problem: We commonly face RS data mismatches

  • upstream: calibration, training, and validation; (data generation)
  • downstream: econometric/policy applications; (data use)

Downstream use should inform upstream modeling.
↳ We don’t train forecasting models on cross-sectional data.
↳ We shouldn’t spatially extrapolate using models built for interpolation.

However, we often implicitly do these things when using remotely sensed data.
↳ RS data producers often don’t have causal inf. in mind.
↳ There aren’t one-size-fits-all datasets… data generation \(\neq\) data application.

Advertised performance: R2 = 0.84
↳ “standard cross validation” randomly samples from the panel;
↳ i.e., ignores spatial/temporal/clustered correlations.

Spatial cross-validation performance: R2 = 0.63
↳ models struggle to predict into new spatial locations…
↳ even when they get spatial lags.

Spatial cross-validation performance without spatial lags: R2 = 0.35
↳ spatial lags take advantage of spatial correlations…
↳ also let data leak from training to testing folds.

ML models are struggling in space: R2 ~ 0.34 (< .84)
↳ Whether we train with spatial or “standard” CV, ML models are struggling
↳ to predict at new spatial locations.
↳ We aren’t learning as much as we think we are where we want to learn.
↳ More spatially explicit learners may be needed…

Related: Boser (2024) shows

  • high total R2 is achievable (.72),
  • even when spatial R2 is quite low (.09).

“Pooled” R2 also rewards explained temporal variation (R2 ≈ .83).

(Akin to Simpson’s paradox.)

Related: Fixed effects can absorb a lot of variation in such predictions.

Summary

Where are we?

Remote sensing changed what is possible for enviro. economics…

  • previously unobserveable variables,
  • coverage where conventional data are sparse,
  • tools to monitor hidden or strategic behavior.

… but seeing is not the same as identifying.

  • predicted variables rather than precise measurements;
  • selection into ground truth;
  • still relies upon conventional (endogenous) monitoring systems.

The next frontier is not just more/better pixels.
↳ improve identification/inference, validation, and monitoring infrastructure.
